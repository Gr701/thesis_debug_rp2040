 \section{Metrics description}

Since there are several candidates for the implementation of the probe, one of the results of this work is the comparison between them. To weigh the different approaches we will define a set of metrics to apply to each of them. Our set will include both quantitative and qualitative metrics, chosen based on the goal of the project, which is to implement a solution used throughout the educational process. While the values of the quantitative metrics will come directly from the tests and measurements, for qualitative metrics each of the team members will decide on a score from 1 to 5 according to their experience and the defined edge cases. The average of those will be used in further considerations.

\subsection{Quantitative metrics}

Quantitative metrics are utilised to assess measurable, objective factors. These metrics are needed to assess the scalability of a solution within the Computer System course setting. They include the following:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Price of the Hardware}: the price of the hardware. That is, the price of the plastic or other materials used for the casing, which would fit the TKJHAT and the probe together, as well as the price of the additional microcontrollers, boards, and wires used in the design. Since tens of debugger units are required by the course, the price of each one is a significant detail.
    
    \item \textbf{Time to Produce}: the time to produce the probe, which includes the printing time of the case for the given probe and the time for soldering and other assembling activities.   
    
    \item \textbf{Physical Size}: the physical size of the final result. That is the size of the casing together with the TKJHAT and the probe. 
    
    \item \textbf{Memory Overhead}: the virtual size as a separate metric. Estimates the memory consumed by the debugger setup on the device being debugged. While it might be one of the most crucial mectrics when working on big and complicated projects, it only takes a secondary position in our research, since most of the projects at the entry-level course are quite simple and compact.
    
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Qualitative metrics}

Qualitative metrics address the human-centric aspects of the platform. The metrics were chosen to evaluate the educational effectiveness and the sustainability of each implementation, ensuring the chosen platform is both intuitive for beginners and reliable for long-term educational use. Qualitative metrics include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State of Maintenance:} how often software is updated, if at all; how many people are behind it and how reliable it is in the long run. It is important since the outcome of our work is planned to be in use for a long time, about seven years, and it is likely, that there will be new bugs found and changes made in the upstream software. In this metric grade 5 means the frequent updates are present, long-term support is likely to continue and a trustworthy entity is developing the solution. Grade 1 means the absence of updates and an unknown developer. 

    \item \textbf{Beginner-friendliness:} one of the most crucial criteria. Estimates how beginner-friendly the setup and the usage of the probe is, how likely are the people, working with the embedded systems for the first time, to make a mistake, how easy it is to explain the workflow. In this metric grade 5 means that solution does not require any configuration by the user and works out of the box with minimal connection needed and a button press. On the other hand, 1 means that there is a complex hardware connection required, where it is easy to fail, and as complex software installation and configuration needed before the debugging can be started.

    \item \textbf{Comfort of Use:} this metric estimates the hardware setup as well as the response speed. We consider speed here, in the qualitative category, as exact milliseconds are not so important to us as the general feel and the smoothness of the process. In contrast to beginner-friendliness, here we assume that the developer is quite familiar with the procedure, and we estimate how comfortable and fluent the day-to-day debugging routine could be with each of the solutions. Here grade 5 means that the developer gets the full potential of the debugger with no additional cost software- or hardware-wise. Grade 1 means the debugger software overhead is always in the way of the development and the hardware setup is bulky and has a lot of connections or other possible failure points, which would slow down the programmer further. 

\end{itemize}

\section{Applying the metrics}

\subsection{Clarification for the qualitative scores}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State of Maintenance:} the Second Pico and SEGGER J-Link received scores of 5.0 due to their support from established commercial entities and frequent updates. At the same time, pico-debug (Core) was assigned a 2.0 rating, as it relies on a single community maintainer and the RP2040 firmware version is archived and no longer supported.
    \item \textbf{Beginner-friendliness:} the pico-debug (Second Core) implementation achieved a 5.0 for requiring no external hardware and simple firmware flashing. In contrast, the Second Pico (3.3) and SEGGER J-Link (2.0) were rated lower due to wiring and firmware complexities.
    \item \textbf{Comfort of Use:} both \textbf{Pico-based} solutions scored 4.0 due to their seamless integration with the custom IDE dashboard and theirs physical size being more optimised for handling, while the SEGGER J-Link scored a 3.0 due to its physical bulk and rigid proprietary software requirements.

\end{itemize}
